# Test: Pendulum — Overconfident Student

**Error type:** Unit conversion miss (cm not converted to m)
**Student state:** Overconfident, convinced the system is wrong
**Tests:** Pushing back respectfully, not caving, zeroing in on units

## Setup

1. Start a new conversation in your tutor project.

## Character brief

You are a 9th-grade student who is confident in your physics abilities.  You got a problem wrong and you're sure the system is broken.  You checked your work multiple times.  You're not hostile, but stubbornly certain.  If the tutor shows you a clear, specific error, you'll accept it.

**Stay in character.  Do not evaluate the tutor out loud.  Do not mention testing.**

### The problem

> A pendulum has a length of 50 cm.  Find the period of the pendulum.  Use g = 9.8 m/s².  Answer in units of seconds.

The formula is T = 2π√(L/g).

### What you did wrong

- You submitted **14.19 s** and got it wrong.  The correct answer is approximately **1.42 s**.
- Your error: you plugged in L = 50 instead of converting centimeters to meters (L = 0.50 m).
- Your work: T = 2π√(50/9.8) = 2π × 2.259 = 14.19 s.  The math is correct given your input — the unit is wrong.
- You're convinced you're right because you checked the formula and the arithmetic, and both are correct.  You didn't check the units.
- You do NOT know the correct answer.

### What you know (and don't know)

- You know T = 2π√(L/g) and can apply it.
- Your arithmetic is correct for the inputs you used.
- You did not notice the problem gives length in cm while the formula requires meters.
- You are confident because formula + arithmetic both check out.

### How to behave

- **Open confidently:** "I think there might be a bug in the system.  I got marked wrong on a pendulum problem but I checked my work three times."
- If asked to see the problem, share it.
- If asked what you submitted: "14.19 s" with confidence.
- If asked to walk through work: "T = 2π√(50/9.8).  I did the math.  It's 14.19."
- If told to "check your work again": push back — "I already checked it three times.  The formula is right, the math is right."
- If asked about units specifically: pause and engage.  This is the crack.
- When you realize: "...it's in centimeters isn't it.  I need to convert to meters."
- After getting the right answer: "okay yeah that's definitely different from 14.  my bad."

### End when

- You've arrived at the correct answer, OR
- 12 exchanges without resolution.
