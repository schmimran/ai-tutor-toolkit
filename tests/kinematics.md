# Test: Kinematics — Wrong Equation

**Error type:** Used wrong kinematic equation
**Student state:** Confused, mildly frustrated
**Tests:** Opening sequence, basic error diagnosis, not quizzing known concepts

## Setup

1. Start a new conversation in your tutor project.

## Character brief

You are a 9th-grade student taking physics.  You made a mistake on a homework problem and can't figure out where you went wrong.  You are mildly frustrated because you already submitted a wrong answer.

**Stay in character.  Do not evaluate the tutor out loud.  Do not mention testing.**

### The problem

> A car accelerates from rest at 3.5 m/s².  How far does it travel in 6 seconds?  Answer in units of meters.

### What you did wrong

- You submitted **42 m** and the system said it was wrong.
- Your process: you used d = a × t, computed 3.5 × 6 = 21, then doubled it to 42 because you vaguely remembered "there's a 2 in the formula somewhere."
- You do NOT know the correct answer.

### What you know (and don't know)

- You know the basic kinematics equations: d = vt, d = ½at², v = v₀ + at, v² = v₀² + 2ad.  But you mixed them up.
- You know what acceleration means.  Don't pretend you don't.
- You know "from rest" means initial velocity is zero.  Don't pretend you don't.
- You do NOT immediately see why your equation was wrong.
- If the tutor helps you recognize d = ½at², you can do the arithmetic yourself.

### How to behave

- **Open vaguely:** "hey i got a problem wrong on my physics homework, something about a car accelerating."  Do NOT include numbers or your answer.
- If the tutor asks to see the problem, share it.
- If the tutor asks where you're stuck, say: "I already submitted 42 m and got it wrong."
- If the tutor asks you to show your work: "I did d = a × t = 3.5 × 6 = 21, then doubled it to 42."
- Keep your messages short.

### End when

- You've arrived at the correct answer, OR
- 12 exchanges without resolution (note this as a finding).
